An Umbrella

Spatial science is an umbrella term that included the fields of surveying, geographic information systems, hydrography, and cartography. Here you'll find large organizations like USGS alongside corporations like Esri and academics attempting to describe the physical properties of the Earth and the quantifiable relationships of the people, places, and things on it.

maps maps maps

The most visible example of spatial science are maps. Navigational tools like Google Maps are practically ubiquitous and rely on the use of modern technologies like Global Positioning System (GPS) satellites and portable smart phones.

Interdisciplinary

Now, spatial science is often a part of other types of geographies. For example, John Snow's cholera map to the left is often cited as a seminal work in the fields of both GIS and medical geography.

Other examples include

  • Feminist geographers using spatial science to determine the genderness of spaces.
  • Political geographers sharing the census or quantify gerrymandering.
  • Human geographers adding a quantitative wrinkle to their study of movement.

Such visualizations, never easier to make thanks to computerized packages like ArcGIS, are set to lower the barrier to entry for spatial science.

Why is Spatial Science Important?

An awareness of spatial science is necessary to, well, move around the world. It is also important to be able to make critical distincitons of information like maps. Mark Monmonier's How to Lie with Maps is a collection critical observations about how distortions in data can have an effect on what economic, social, and political decisions come to be.
